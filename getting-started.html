Getting Started

A practical, step-by-step guide to building and adapting two-button systems for access, tools, and games. More updates coming soon.

⚠️ Important Disclaimer

We are not medical professionals and our guides do not replace therapy or medical advice. Please collaborate with licensed SLPs, OTs, or Assistive Technology specialists whenever possible.

What This Page Is About

Families ask us how to use technology like Ben's. This guide focuses on tools and games first. We also share a simple keyboard and phrase board so users can communicate while they explore games independently.

We recommend exploring OpenAAC.org for broad AAC knowledge. Our focus is teaching you how to use AI to tailor or build your own tools and games when off-the-shelf options fall short.

How To Get Started

Step 1. Understand who you are building for

Begin with the person. What do they want to do or say. What movements can they perform comfortably and consistently. How do they show preference, frustration, or excitement.

If communication is unclear, start with a yes and no method, then expand.

Step 2. Identify input methods

Input is how your loved one interacts with technology. The goal is a reliable, comfortable, repeatable movement that triggers an action.

Neutral brand note: we are not affiliated with any brand. The examples below are for general guidance. Availability and designs vary, so research what fits your user.

  • Switches and buttons - Jelly Bean, Buddy Button, low profile thumb switches. DIY options exist.
  • Head tracking - camera based options like SmartNav or TrackIR, or open source like Camera Mouse or Opentrack. Often combined with a switch to select.
  • Eye tracking - Tobii and EyeTech are common. Works best with stable posture.
  • Facial gesture - blinks, smiles, brow raises with software that interprets a webcam feed.
  • Joysticks and adaptive controllers - Xbox Adaptive Controller, Logitech Adaptive Gaming Kit, and specialty joysticks.
  • Sip and puff - breath based devices such as sip and puff switches or mouth controlled joysticks.
  • EMG and EEG - muscle sensor and brainwave systems for advanced or experimental setups.

How to choose: start with the simplest movement your user can repeat without strain. Test comfort over time. Prioritize stable positioning.

Step 3. Connect inputs to a computer or console

Common paths include USB switch interfaces, DIY microcontroller adapters, the Xbox Adaptive Controller, or the Microsoft Adaptive Hub. Many switches use 3.5 mm mono jacks. Mounting and positioning are just as important as the device itself.

Step 4. Try free or low cost software

Test before you build. Good options include AsTeRICS Grid, CoughDrop, and Cboard. Our own tools are available free:

  • NARBE Keyboard - a simple predictive keyboard for one or two button access.
  • NARBE Phrase Board - an editable phrase board for daily communication.

Start simple, learn what feels natural, then expand.

Step 5. Fine tune and adjust

  • Scan speed and rhythm
  • Debounce timing to prevent double hits
  • Audio feedback or text to speech
  • High contrast themes and larger targets
  • Auto scan versus manual scan
  • Switch placement and posture
  • Short sessions to avoid fatigue

Step 6. Use AI to tailor tools

You can personalize interfaces without prior coding. Try a clear prompt:

Create a two button scanning menu. Space moves to the next option and Enter selects. Add text to speech that reads each option aloud.

Iterate in small steps. Save working versions often. See the Resources page for upcoming walkthroughs.

Step 7. Try and adapt existing systems - starting with Ben's

Ben's software is a fully functional two button system you can try for free on our GitHub. It is a good sandbox to learn how scanning works and to gauge comfort.

  1. Visit the NARBE Foundation GitHub - link coming soon.
  2. Follow the Installation Guide - link coming soon.
  3. Connect two switches mapped to Space and Enter.
  4. Run on a Windows PC or tablet, then explore menus, games, and voice settings.

If it feels like too much, start smaller. If it fits, adapt and expand with AI or your own code. The system is modular so you can disable menus or add new tools.

Step 8. Build accessible games

Games are a fun way to practice interaction. Focus on logic, turn based play, and choice driven stories that do not require fast reaction time.

Visit the Gaming Hub for 2 Button Access. It is fully independent, mapped to Spacebar and Return, and always free to use and adapt.

Find templates and examples on the Community Tools and Games page. External hubs like OneSwitch, SpecialEffect, and Game Accessibility Guidelines offer deep catalogs and best practices.

Step 9. Learn from the community

OpenAAC is a great place to learn about AAC ecosystems. AbleGamers and SpecialEffect share real world setups. OneSwitch curates switch friendly software. Use these to avoid reinventing the wheel, then build what you cannot find.

Step 10. Keep learning and sharing

Join our community, ask questions, and share what works so others can build on it.
