Getting Started

A practical, step-by-step guide to building and adapting two-button systems for access, tools, and games. More updates coming soon.

⚠️ Important Disclaimer

We are not medical professionals and our guides do not replace therapy or medical advice. Please collaborate with licensed SLPs, OTs, or Assistive Technology specialists whenever possible.

What This Page Is About

Families ask us how to use technology like Ben's. This guide focuses on tools and games first. We also share a simple keyboard and phrase board so users can communicate while they explore games independently.

We recommend exploring OpenAAC.org for broad AAC knowledge. Our focus is teaching you how to use AI to tailor or build your own tools and games when off-the-shelf options fall short.

How To Get Started

Before diving in, please note:

NARBE Foundation is not an AAC manufacturer or therapy provider. There are already many excellent AAC systems available — such as CoughDrop, Proloquo2Go, TD Snap, Grid 3, and TouchChat.

Our focus is helping families who haven't found success with those systems, or who want to build and adapt tools themselves. We offer guidance, examples, and some simple AAC-style options when commercial tools don't fit your needs.

Everyone's journey is unique. What worked for our brother Ben may not work for you. Ben already understood language, spelling, and technology before he became nonverbal and quadriplegic. Progress depends on many factors, so please view this guide as inspiration, not instruction.

We make no guarantees — your mileage may vary.

Step 1. Understand who you're building for

Start with the person, not the technology.

Ask yourself:

  • What do they want to do or say?
  • What movements can they do comfortably and consistently?
  • How do they show preference, frustration, or excitement?

If communication is unclear, begin with something simple like yes and no, then expand slowly.

Step 2. Identify possible input methods

Input is how your loved one interacts with technology. The goal is a comfortable, repeatable movement that triggers an action.

Neutral brand note: we're not affiliated with any company. These examples are for general guidance.

  • Switches and buttons – examples include Jelly Bean, Buddy Button, or low-profile thumb switches. DIY options exist.
  • Head tracking – camera-based tools such as SmartNav or TrackIR, or open-source options like Camera Mouse or Opentrack. Often paired with a switch.
  • Eye tracking – Tobii and EyeTech are common. Works best with stable posture.
  • Facial gesture control – uses a webcam to interpret blinks, smiles, or eyebrow raises.
  • Joysticks and adaptive controllers – Xbox Adaptive Controller, Logitech Adaptive Gaming Kit, or other custom joysticks.
  • Sip-and-puff – breath-based control through sip-and-puff switches or mouth joysticks.
  • EMG / EEG – advanced or experimental systems that detect muscle or brain activity.

How to choose:

Start with the simplest movement your user can repeat comfortably. Test for fatigue. Stable positioning often matters more than the device itself.

Step 3. Connect inputs to a computer or tablet

Common connection paths include:

  • USB switch interfaces or DIY microcontroller adapters
  • Xbox Adaptive Controller or Microsoft Adaptive Hub

Most switches use 3.5 mm mono jacks. Proper mounting and positioning are just as important as the hardware.

Step 4. Try free or low-cost software

Before you build anything, experiment with existing tools:

  • AsTeRICS Grid – open-source AAC system for creating customizable communication grids.
  • CoughDrop – cloud-based AAC platform with sharing features.
  • Cboard – free web-based AAC with symbol libraries.
  • OpenAAC.org – learn about AAC systems, frameworks, and open-source projects.

These tools let you explore scanning, speech output, and layout options before you design your own.

Step 5. Fine-tune and adjust

Once you've found a working setup, take time to refine it:

  • Adjust scan speed and timing
  • Add debounce filters to prevent double hits
  • Enable audio or text-to-speech feedback
  • Use high-contrast visuals and large buttons
  • Try both auto-scan and manual-scan modes
  • Adjust switch placement and posture
  • Keep sessions short to avoid fatigue

Small changes often make a big difference.

Step 6. Use AI or simple code to personalize tools

There's a lot that goes into AI-assisted building, but it always starts with a framework and builds upward from there. Think of it like stacking blocks: start simple, test, refine, and keep adding layers until it feels right. The process is all about testing and retesting to find what truly works.

You don't need programming experience. Start with a clear goal and describe it to an AI tool such as ChatGPT, Gemini, or Copilot. For example:

"Create a two-button scanning menu where Space moves to the next option and Enter selects. Add text-to-speech that reads each option aloud."

Then, grow from there:

  • Add a back button or predictive text feature.
  • Adjust scan timing or visual highlights.
  • Include sound feedback for each selection.
  • Save versions that work before making new changes.

If you're unsure where to start, use AI as a thought partner. Ask questions, brainstorm, and have it explain concepts in simple language. AI can help you imagine what's possible and break ideas down into achievable steps.

If you decide to try basic programming, begin with something easy and visual — like HTML, CSS, and a little JavaScript. These languages let you build simple tools that run directly in a web browser (no installs required). AI can generate small examples you can copy, edit, and learn from. You'll be surprised how quickly you can make menus, phrase boards, or scanning systems this way.

The goal isn't to build something perfect. It's to make something that works for your loved one, even if it's small. We'll continue adding AI-based walkthroughs and templates on our Resources page to help families learn and experiment safely.

Step 7. Explore accessible games

Games can build confidence and motivation while reinforcing input control. Look for turn-based, logic, or story-driven experiences that don't rely on speed or reflexes.

Useful sites and organizations:

  • NARBE House | Benny's Hub – Games are going to be uploaded and updated - these are 2 button game access with a simple AAC and media browser for anyone to use.
  • OneSwitch.org.uk – switch-accessible games and DIY resources.
  • SpecialEffect – nonprofit helping people access games through adaptive setups.
  • Game Accessibility Guidelines – practical advice for inclusive game design.

Step 8. Learn from the community

There's a large global network of people sharing ideas, code, and solutions:

  • OpenAAC – learn about open AAC ecosystems and research.
  • AbleGamers and SpecialEffect – examples of adaptive technology in real-world use.
  • OneSwitch – curated database of switch-friendly tools and games.

These communities are great places to ask questions and see what others are building.

Step 9. Keep learning and sharing

Join the NARBE Foundation community to ask questions, show your progress, and learn from others. Each story helps someone else take their first step. What works for you might inspire another family tomorrow.

Keep exploring, keep testing, and remember — accessibility grows when we build it together.
